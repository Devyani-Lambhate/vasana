Research · Project 01

AI Datacenter
Environmental Impact

AI data centers consume substantial amounts of energy and water, often even where local communities still lack reliable access to clean water. We aim to map all data centers and assess their relationship to regional water scarcity and energy availability.

01 · Carbon & Energy

Power consumption and carbon intensity

Global datacenters consumed an estimated 240–340 TWh of electricity in 2022, roughly equivalent to the annual electricity use of the United Kingdom. AI-specific workloads — training and inference — represent a rapidly growing share of this total.

Carbon intensity varies dramatically by geography: a model trained using coal-heavy grid electricity in parts of Asia may produce 30× more CO₂ than the same model trained on renewable-powered infrastructure in Norway.

02 · Water Usage

Cooling demand and water scarcity

Datacenters use water for cooling at scale. A single large hyperscale facility can consume millions of litres per day — drawing from local aquifers and river systems, often in regions already facing water stress.

Training a single large language model has been estimated to require billions of litres of water when cooling is accounted for across the full energy chain. This figure is rarely disclosed voluntarily.

03 · E-Waste & Hardware

Chip lifecycle and electronic waste

AI's hardware demands have compressed GPU replacement cycles to 2–3 years as model architectures evolve faster than previous compute generations. Each cycle generates electronic waste containing rare earth elements, lead, and other hazardous materials.

Global e-waste recycling rates remain below 25%. The majority of decommissioned AI hardware enters informal recycling chains or landfill, with significant downstream environmental consequences.

04 · Provider Scorecards

Transparency and accountability ratings

Major cloud and AI providers vary significantly in the quality, completeness, and verifiability of their environmental disclosures. Some publish detailed PUE, WUE, and carbon metrics. Others offer only high-level commitments with no auditable underlying data.

Vasana rates providers across four dimensions: disclosure completeness, methodology transparency, progress against stated targets, and third-party verification.
